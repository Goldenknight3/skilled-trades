The Trade


Industrial Mechanic Millwright

The Job

An Industrial Mechanic Millwright is responsable for maintaining and fixing factory machines. They must know all sorts of different mechanisms, including pneumatics, hydraulics, electric motors, combustion engines, gearboxes, and more. They need to know the basics of machining their own parts, using various tools.

So these people have a lot to learn, and a lot to do! Lets dive deeper, into the work duties, work conditions, aprenticeship, education required, salary, and the pros and cons.
I will also mention a reflection on my opinion, and a list of sources.

Work Duties

Industrial Mechanic Millwrights are responsable for the repair, maintenance and checkups for the machinery used in factories. They have the very important job of enusuring that all the machines in a factory run smoothly. If there is a breakdown, they will be called first. They are also expected to mill their own parts, because factories cannot wait weeks for a part to arrive.

Work Conditions

Industrial Mechanic Millwrights often work around 40 hours per week. Sometimes, they need to work on call in order to restore machine breakdowns.Sometimes mechanics need to move to remote locations.
Since they need to work in factories to repair equipment, they work in noisy, dirty enviroments. Sometimes work must be done outdoors, exposing them to potentially bad weather.
Factories also can have many dangers to them, like falling objects and very powerful moving parts. This is why industrial mechanics need to be extensivley trained in safety.

Aprenticeship

The code for the Industrial Mechanic Millwright Aprenticeship Program is 433A. The program generally takes four years to complete, and is made up of 7,280 hours of on the job learning/working, and 720 hours of in-school training. Mohawk College offers this as a "earn while you learn" program. You must need at least high school diploma to apply for the program.
Some important skills needed for the job are :

  • Protecting self and others
  • Communicating
  • Using drawings and schematics
  • Using hand and power tools
  • Using machine tools

Education Required

The education needed to become a Industrial Mechanic Millwright is as follows:

  1. Completion of a secondary school program. (High school degree)
  2. Completion of the aprenticeship program mentionned above.
  3. Some specific industries require extra training in their fields.
Some extra degrees you can obtain to increase your quality as an Industrial Mechanic Millwright:
  1. Endorsement from Red Seal Canada: Requires passing their examination.
  2. Industrial Mechanic Millwright trade certification
  3. Construction Millwright trade certification is available, but only in Quebec and Ontario.

Salary

Industrial Mechanic Millwrights make $79,000 per year two years after being certified. Since this that would just be the beginning, it could rise to over $100,000 per year.

Pros and Cons

Pros Cons
  • It is very easiy to start earning as a millwright. Upon completing grade twelve, you can sign up for the aprenticeship program, and after two years, you will be earning $79,000 per year.
  • If you are interested in enginnering, which most of this tech class is, then this job is perfect as it incorporates enginnering into your daily job.
  • You do not even need to complete the official aprenticeship program. Becoming an Inudstrial Mechanic Millwrights is a free trade, meaning that you can get educated anywhere. So even if the aprenticeship program is not available for whatever reason, you can still get a job via other kinds of education.
  • Even as an experienced millright, there is a good chance you won't get paid over $100,000 per year.
  • Jobs can be very dirty or noisy: For example, you may have to take apart a conveyor belt, which may be covered in dirty lubricant. Noisy machinery can also be detrimental to your mental health/the physical health of your ears.
  • Your peers may not respect this carrer path as it is a physical skilled trade, and these are not very well respected among most parents today. Many parents expect their children to become the designers of these machines, not the maintence of them.

Would I work this job?

I think if I don't get into any univeristies, then I will honestly consider this job, at least to survive. Considering I could start making a very nice wage for only two years of extra education, I could work for a little bit before trying to return to university.
